Over the past quarter, three patterns have emerged across our technical diligence engagements that are materially affecting how we scope AI-related assessments.

First, the distinction between "AI-enabled" and "AI-native" is becoming a critical diligence finding. Companies that bolted conversational AI onto existing workflows present fundamentally different integration risk profiles than those built around ML pipelines from the start. The former looks like a feature; the latter looks like architecture. The implications for post-acquisition integration timelines differ by 6-12 months.

Second, data pipeline maturity is surfacing as a more reliable indicator of AI capability than model sophistication. We are seeing portfolio companies with impressive demo capabilities that fall apart under production data quality scrutiny. The diligence question has shifted from "what models do you use" to "show me your data lineage and validation layers."

Third, AI talent retention is becoming a deal-specific risk factor that belongs in the investment memo, not just the HR section of the diligence report. The market for ML engineers with production experience (not just research backgrounds) has tightened considerably, and we have seen two deals in the past quarter where key technical talent departed within 90 days of close.

For PE buyers, the practical implication is that AI-focused diligence now warrants a dedicated workstream rather than a subsection of the standard technology assessment. The technical and business risks are distinct enough to justify the additional scope.
